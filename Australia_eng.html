Australia

A dynamic and contrasting place, where cities and nature have their own unique energy.

Characteristics of Australian Citizens

  • "No worries” lifestyle

    Australians are known for their relaxed approach to life. The phrase "No worries!” is heard everywhere – from casual chats to serious situations. It doesn’t just mean "don’t worry,” but reflects a generally positive attitude: even if something goes wrong, it’s not the end of the world. That’s one reason why Australians are considered among the happiest people on Earth.

  • The spirit of friendship

    Australians are very friendly and open – they often call strangers "mate” and can easily strike up a conversation with anyone. Whether in a shop queue or sitting on the beach – everyone feels welcome. Friendship is highly valued in Australia, and people are always ready to help and support each other, creating a strong sense of community.

  • Outdoor lifestyle

    Australians love nature and being outdoors. With such a climate, it would be a sin to stay locked inside! Beaches, national parks, lakes, and mountains are a part of daily life. They enjoy picnics, surfing, hiking, and simply evening strolls. Even in city centers, you’ll see people sitting on the grass and soaking up the sun.

  • Barefoot – why not?

    Australians aren’t too worried about dress codes – shoes are often seen as optional. Whether you’re going to a shop or a café, no one will be surprised if you show up barefoot. It’s a symbol of freedom and comfort that perfectly reflects their way of life.

  • Australian humor

    Australians have a unique sense of humor – it’s dry, sarcastic, and sometimes even a bit rough, but always good-natured. They love to joke about themselves and others, but never with bad intentions. If an Aussie teases you playfully – take it as a sign that they like you!

  • Tough as rocks

    Australians are tough and resilient – living in a country where nature often throws challenges at you teaches adaptability. Heatwaves, floods, dangerous animals, or long distances between cities – all of this demands strength and patience. That’s why Aussies are often seen as one of the "toughest” nations in the world.

  • Everyone is equal

    Australians value equality – it doesn’t matter if you’re a millionaire or a student, everyone is simply called "mate.” They dislike hierarchy and excessive formality, so even people in high positions often communicate in a down-to-earth way. This creates a very open and friendly society.

  • Love for sport

    Australians are absolutely mad about sports. Cricket, rugby, football, surfing – it’s not just entertainment, it’s part of their culture. Sporting events are real celebrations, where people gather, cheer, share emotions, and enjoy togetherness. And they don’t just watch – they actively take part, too.

  • "Shoey” – weird but fun tradition

    Australians have a wild and unique tradition – drinking beer from a shoe, known as a "shoey.” It’s usually done during celebrations, concerts, or big victories. While it might seem strange to outsiders, in Australia it’s seen as a fun and bold gesture.

Holidays

  • Australia Day

    Australia Day is celebrated on January 26th with fireworks, concerts, and citizenship ceremonies, though for some it’s a controversial date due to colonization.

  • Good Friday

    Good Friday is a Christian day of reflection and fasting before Easter, marked by church services.

  • ANZAC Day

    ANZAC Day, observed on April 25th, honors Australian and New Zealand soldiers with dawn services and parades.

  • Boxing Day

    Boxing Day, celebrated on December 26th, is known for huge sales, sporting events, and quality time with family.

Phrases

Here’s some often used phrases in Australia! These phrases will help you not to get lost during conversations. However, use them with cautions, because if you get carried away, it can come across as rude.

"Barbie"

Meaning: barbecue, a cookout with a grill.

Usage: "Throw some shrimp on the barbie!"

Barbecues are very popular in Australia, especially in the summer.

A tip! Don’t confuse it with the doll "Barbie" – it’s a completely different thing!

"Brekkie"

Meaning: breakfast (short ver.).

Usage: "Let's grab some brekkie before work."

Australians love having breakfast at cafes, especially avocado toast!

A tip! If you’re in Australia, saying "brekkie" will make you sound more local.

"Footy"

Meaning: football, soccer or rugby.

Usage: "Are you watching the footy tonight?"

In different regions, "footy" can mean different sports – in Victoria, it’s usually AFL, while in New South Wales, it’s rugby.

A tip! When in Australia, ask the locals which sport they mean!

"Selfie"

Meaning: a self-portrait, taking a photo of oneself.

Usage: "Let's take a selfie together!"

Although the whole world uses the term "selfie", it actually originated in Australia!

A tip! To sound even more Australian, say "selfie stick" as "selfie sticko".

"Polly"

Meaning: politician (short ver.).

Usage: "The pollies are making big promises."

Australians often use this word with a bit of irony or skepticism.

A tip! If you want to joke about politics, this phrase works perfectly.

"Bizzo"

Meaning: business, matters, affair.

Usage: "What's the bizzo with that project?"

This word is often used in everyday speech.

A tip! It might sound a bit old-fashioned – not every young Aussie uses this term.

"Ambo"

Meaning: paramedic, ambulance officer.

Usage: "Call an ambo right now!"

Australians often shorten job titles: "firie" (firefighter), "copper" (police officer).

A tip! If you’re in trouble in Australia, this word might come in handy.

"Preggo"

Meaning: pregnant (short ver.).

Usage: "She's preggo with twins!"

This is a friendly and informal term, but some women might find it a bit strange.

A tip! Only use it if you know the person won’t be offended – some may find it too casual.

"Arvo"

Meaning: afternoon (short ver.).

Usage: "We should meet this arvo."

This is one of the most recognizable Aussie terms.

A tip! If you say "arvo" instead of "afternoon", you'll instantly sound like a local!

"No worries"

Meaning: "no problem", "you’re welcome".

Usage: "Thanks for your help! - No worries!"

Australians often say "no worries" instead of "you're welcome".

A tip! Use this phrase as much as possible – it’s very Aussie!

"G'day"

Meaning: "Good day" (short ver.).

Usage: "G'day, mate! How ya going?"

This is one of the most recognizable Aussie greetings.

A tip! If you're in Australia, try greeting someone this way – locals will appreciate it!

"Dog's breakfast"

Meaning: a mess, chaotic situation.

Usage: "Your room looks like a dog's breakfast!"

Australians love using animal metaphors.

A tip! Use it when something looks really bad!

"Give it a burl"

Meaning: "Try it", "give it a go".

Usage: "Never tried surfing? Give it a burl!"

The word "burl" isn’t widely used outside of Australia, so this phrase is very local.

A tip! If you want to encourage a friend to try something new, this phrase is perfect!

"Good on ya"

Meaning: "Well done", "good job".

Usage: "Helped your friend move? Good on ya!"

This phrase is very friendly and positive, commonly used in Australia.

A tip! Add "mate" to sound even more Aussie: "Good on ya, mate!".

Food

  • ANZAC biscuit

    "ANZAC biscuit" – These are oat biscuits with golden syrup and coconut, created for ANZAC soldiers during World War I. They are crunchy on the outside and slightly soft inside.

  • Macadamia nuts

    "Macadamia nuts" – These are creamy-flavored nuts that grow in Australia, often eaten roasted, salted, or used in desserts and baked goods.

  • Damper

    "Damper" – A traditional Australian bread baked on hot coals without yeast, often served with butter or syrup. It’s an ancient traveler or pioneer food.

  • Weet-bix

    "Weet-bix" – A breakfast cereal made from whole wheat, often eaten with milk and fruits. It’s a popular and healthy choice in Australia.

  • Lamingtons

    "Lamingtons" – Square sponge cakes covered in chocolate and rolled in coconut. It’s one of Australia’s most iconic desserts.

  • Lamb leg roast

    "Lamb leg roast" – A slow-cooked lamb leg, often served with roasted vegetables and gravy. It’s a classic Sunday roast in Australia.

  • Iced Vovo

    "Iced Vovo" – A biscuit topped with pink icing, jam, and coconut flakes. It’s a sweet and nostalgic snack loved in Australia.

  • Barbecued snags

    "Barbecued snags" – Australian sausages grilled on the BBQ, often served with bread and onions. They are an essential part of Australian BBQ culture.

  • Prawn cocktail

    "Prawn cocktail" – A light appetizer of cold prawns served with cocktail sauce and salad. It’s a classic party dish in Australia.

  • Tim Tam

    "Tim Tam" – Chocolate-covered biscuits with a creamy filling, often enjoyed with tea or coffee. One of Australia’s most popular sweets.

  • SAO biscuit

    "SAO biscuit" – Light and crispy crackers often eaten with butter, cheese, or Vegemite. It’s a long-standing Australian snack.

  • Cherry ripe

    "Cherry ripe" – A chocolate bar filled with cherries and coconut. It’s one of the oldest and most beloved sweets in Australia.

  • Fairy bread

    "Fairy bread" – Slices of white bread with butter and colorful sprinkles. A popular children’s birthday snack in Australia.

  • Pigs in blanket

    "Pigs in blanket" – Sausages wrapped in dough and baked until golden. A popular party snack in Australia.

  • Meat pies

    "Meat pies" – Small pie-shaped pastries with a meat filling and gravy. One of Australia’s favorite fast food choices.

  • Crocodile

    "Crocodile" – A meat dish made from crocodile, often grilled or stewed. The meat has a mild taste similar to chicken and is often used in cold appetizers.

  • Vegemite on toast

    "Vegemite on toast" – A popular Australian breakfast dish where Vegemite, a salty spread, is smeared on toast with butter.

  • Vanilla slice

    "Vanilla slice" – A sweet dessert with vanilla custard and a flaky pastry base. It’s very popular in Australia and often served cold.

  • Cheese and bacon roll

    "Cheese and bacon roll" – Pastries filled with cheese and bacon, often served as a quick snack or a light lunch.

  • Kangaroo

    "Kangaroo" – A meat dish made from kangaroo, which has a tender yet slightly strong flavor. It’s often grilled or roasted.

  • Crab Sticks

    "Crab sticks" – Imitation crab meat sticks, often used in salads, snacks, or with pasta.

  • Hamburger with beetroot

    "Hamburger with beetroot" – A traditional Australian hamburger that includes sweet and juicy beetroot.

The Don'ts in Australia

Here’s some things you should never do, if you want to blend in and avoid uncomfortable situations. Follow these tips to make a great impression!

Don't: Why? What to do instead:
Littering In Australia, great care is taken for the environment, and littering is considered a serious offense. Always dispose of trash in designated areas.
Disrespecting local cultures The Aboriginal culture in Australia is very important, and disrespecting it can be offensive. Show respect for local traditions and history.
Mocking the accent Australians take pride in their accent, and mimicking it can be offensive. Be respectful and avoid mimicking local speech patterns.
Forgetting to show gratitude Australians value friendliness, and a simple "thank you" in everyday situations is appreciated. Always thank someone when they help you or provide a service.
Leaving tips Tipping is not common in Australia and may seem odd. Pay the amount stated, unless the service was exceptional.
Complaining about the heat Australians are used to hot weather and often don't make a big deal about the temperature. Prepare for the heat and try not to complain to the locals.
Asking about Starbucks Australians take pride in their local cafes and coffee culture. Instead of looking for Starbucks, try the local coffee shops.
